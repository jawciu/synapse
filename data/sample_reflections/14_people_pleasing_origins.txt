I've been reflecting on why I can never say no. Growing up, I was the "easy child." My older brother had ADHD and took up all the oxygen in the room — meltdowns, school calls, arguments at dinner. I learned that the way to get love was to be invisible, to not cause problems, to anticipate what everyone needed before they asked. Mum would say "thank God for you, at least one of my kids is easy." That became my identity. Even now, when my colleague asks me to take on extra work, there's a part of me that panics — if I say no, I'll become the difficult one, and difficult people get abandoned. The people-pleasing isn't really about being nice. It's a survival strategy I built when I was seven years old to make sure I wouldn't be forgotten.
